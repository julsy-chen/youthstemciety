## Overview

Nanotechnology is a broad, interdisciplinary field of science and technology that studies, manipulates, and manufactures materials at the atomic and molecular level, ranging from 1 to 100 nanometers. To provide a sense of scale, a human fingernail grows about one nanometer every second, and large viruses measure 100 nanometers. The term "nanomaterials" is commonly used to describe nanoscale structures such as nanoparticles, nanotubes, shells, and quantum dots.

## Unique Properties at the Nanoscale

Nanotechnology exhibits high potential due to the unique behaviour of nanoscale materials compared to their bulk forms. Their behaviour is a result of two principal factors.

The first factor is **quantum mechanical effects**. With nanomaterials having sizes similar to fundamental particles such as electrons and photons, quantum mechanics leads to new physics and chemistry. For example, the colour of a particle can change with its size since the arrangement of atoms reflects light differently.

The second factor is the **high surface-to-volume ratio**. A particle's size is inversely related to the number of atoms located on their surface. Nanomaterials have large surface areas, making the particles more reactive since they can easily interact with other materials. The increased reactivity can be beneficial (e.g. increased antioxidant activity or catalytic efficiency) or detrimental (e.g. increased toxicity or oxidative stress on cells).

## Classification of Nanomaterials

Nanomaterials can be categorized based on their origin and composition:

- **Natural nanomaterials** occur naturally in the environment or in living organisms; examples include volcanic ash, smoke, hemoglobin molecules, and the nanoscale structures on peacock feathers.
- **Artificial nanomaterials** result from human-made objects or processes, such as by-products like vehicle exhaust and pollution.
- **Intentionally produced nanomaterials** are human-made materials specifically engineered for certain properties. They are grouped into four main types:
  - **Fullerenes** — different molecular forms of carbon
  - **Metal-Based Nanomaterials**
  - **Dendrimers** — complex, branched nanoparticles with a core and multiple shells
  - **Nanocomposites** — materials that combine nanoparticles with other nanomaterials or bulk materials

## Nanofabrication and Tools

When constructing nanoscale materials and devices, two primary strategies are utilized:

- The **top-down approach** is the dominant method used to create computer chips and microelectronics, involving carving or etching nanoscale features from larger, bulk materials.
- The **bottom-up approach** is inspired by biological systems, and involves assembling products atom-by-atom or molecule-by-molecule, often using chemical and physical forces.

The advancement of nanotechnology also relies on specialized tools such as microscopes to visualize and manipulate individual atoms.

## Applications Across Sectors

As of 2025, nanotechnology is being applied or researched for use in nearly every major industry:

- **Information Technology** — Computer hard drives with smaller and faster transistors are being manufactured using the magnetic properties of nanotechnology.
- **Medicine and Healthcare** — Applications include targeted drug delivery systems, advanced diagnostic tools and bioassays using quantum dots, antimicrobial silver nanoparticles in bandages, and tissue engineering scaffolds.
- **Environment** — Nanotechnology leads to more efficient and affordable solar cells, stronger and lighter wind turbine blades, nano-fueled additives for efficient combustion, nanofiltration for water purification, and magnetic nanoparticles for oil spill cleanup.
- **Forestry and Agriculture** — Wood treatments involving nanotechnology build UV, moisture, and decay resistance; nanoemulsion pesticides support uniform application; and paper quality is improved.
- **Automotive and Aerospace** — Nanotechnology is utilized to create lightweight and stronger materials for greater fuel efficiency, rechargeable battery systems, and sensors.

## Health, Safety, and Environmental Concerns

Unusual properties of nanomaterials lead to significant uncertainty about their potential risks to human health and the environment.

A major concern is that workers involved in the manufacture and application of nanomaterials are directly exposed to the new technology. Research is ongoing into nanomaterials' primary routes of entry into the human body:

- **Inhalation** — Nanoparticles can be deposited throughout the respiratory tract and may enter the bloodstream, distributing to the liver, heart, spleen, and other organs.
- **Skin absorption** — Nanomaterials may cross the skin, potentially accumulating around hair follicles and reaching deeper levels.
- **Ingestion** — Certain nanomaterials tend to pass through the gastrointestinal tract.

Additionally, nanomaterials introduce toxicological concerns due to their unique chemical properties, which may differ from their larger, "normal" counterparts. As a result, workplace control measures must be followed when working with nanomaterials, including:

- Elimination/substitution
- Engineering controls (e.g. exhaust ventilation systems with HEPA filters)
- Administrative controls (e.g. education and training in safe handling)
- Use of personal protective equipment

## Historical Context and Key Milestones

While modern nanotechnology is a recent field, nanoscale materials have been used throughout history. In 4th Century Rome, the Lycurgus Cup was created using gold and silver nanoparticles in glass, causing it to change colour depending on the light source. Similarly, medieval Europe used gold and silver nanoparticles to create vibrant stained-glass windows.

### 1959 — The Theoretical Foundation
American physicist Richard Feynman presented a lecture called *"There's Plenty of Room at the Bottom"*, proposing that the laws of physics do not prevent the manipulation of matter atom-by-atom.

### 1981 — Scanning Tunneling Microscope
The Scanning Tunneling Microscope was invented by German physicist Gerd Binnig and Swiss physicist Heinrich Rohrer. The device marked the beginning of modern nanotechnology as it allowed scientists to image the position of individual atoms on a surface for the first time in history.

### 1985 — Discovery of Buckminsterfullerene
Researchers Robert F. Curl Jr., Harold W. Kroto, and Richard E. Smalley discovered a new form of pure carbon: buckminsterfullerene, nicknamed "buckyballs." Buckyballs are fundamental nanomaterials, consisting of 60 carbon atoms arranged in a hollow sphere of interlocking hexagons and pentagons.

### 1991 — Discovery of Carbon Nanotubes
Carbon nanotubes were identified by Japanese physicist and inventor Sumio Iijima. They are sheets of graphene rolled into long, hollow tubes, and possess extraordinary mechanical and electronic properties (e.g. being harder than diamond yet more flexible than rubber).

## Conclusion

Nanotechnology represents a transformative shift in science and technology, leading to revolutionary solutions in medicine, electronics, and environmental conservation. From targeted drug delivery and high-efficiency solar cells to advanced water filtration and "smart" industrial materials, nanomaterials have high potential to enhance human health and sustainability. However, as studies into nanotechnology are still ongoing, the challenges that come along with it must be addressed, such as its potential health risks and safety. Ultimately, nanotechnology can be successfully integrated into mainstream society if further innovation and scientific study is conducted.
