## Introduction

As a child, you may have been told to stay away from stray animals such as dogs and cats, and wild animals such as coyotes. These warnings might have specified what to do in the case of encountering such an animal, either avoiding physical contact or seeking shelter. If you asked adults why this was necessary, they might have said something along the lines of animals passing infections to humans, and they are right. Rabies is a severe viral disease, transmitted through saliva, and affects mammals.

## The Disease Itself

Rabies is a viral disease, meaning it is caused by a virus — the lyssavirus of the Rhabdoviridae family — and uses the host's cells to reproduce. Viruses are a type of germ that do not have cells, and therefore cannot reproduce on their own. Rabies contraction happens by infected saliva breaking the skin. This usually happens through a bite or scratch, but can also happen by saliva entering an open wound, or the eyes, nose, mouth, and ears.

Symptoms appear after an incubation period, which typically lasts 1–3 months. Once symptoms appear, death occurs after 1–2 weeks, and it is almost impossible to receive treatment after symptoms appear. The virus is almost always fatal, killing victims through different methods, accompanied by other symptoms that vary depending on the type of rabies.

## Types of Rabies

### Paralytic ("Dumb") Rabies

Paralytic rabies makes up 20% of total rabies cases, and is often misdiagnosed due to a lack of awareness. It causes paralysis spreading from the site of the infection. Other specific symptoms include urinary incontinence and weakening muscles. Patients die because the paralysis eventually causes heart and respiratory failure.

### Encephalitic ("Furious") Rabies

Also known as "furious" rabies, this is the publicized type of rabies, often depicted in media, and makes up the remaining 80% of cases. It is called "furious" because it infamously causes aggression, lack of coordination, and hyperactivity — essentially making its victims act uncharacteristically, which is why it's much easier to identify than paralytic rabies. Encephalitis is the swelling of the brain and nervous system, which is how furious rabies kills.

Once the nervous system swells, it causes **hydrophobia** (fear of water). The virus affects muscles that control contractions in the neck, making them spasm painfully and making the act of swallowing too painful. This is why rabies victims "foam at the mouth" — the inability to swallow causes saliva to build up. So it is not an actual fear of water, but the literal incapacity to swallow, most associated with water as it is more important to survival than food. In later stages, victims appear afraid of water in any form, whether for drinking, cleaning, or showering.

For the same reasons, victims develop **aerophobia** (fear of air), as the muscle spasms also make breathing painful, and eventually victims become very uncomfortable or afraid of drafts of air.

## Prodromal Symptoms

These are the early signs of rabies that appear during the incubation phase, and apply to both paralytic and furious rabies. They begin with a tingling sensation from the infection site, followed by flu-like symptoms including sore throat, headaches, and fatigue. It is only during the incubation phase that a patient can seek treatment — it must be done before neurological symptoms begin, or the patient is essentially already beyond saving.

## Treatment

### Pre-Exposure Vaccine

There are rabies vaccines that can be given both pre-exposure and post-exposure. In Canada, the vaccine is recommended for high-risk groups such as vets and animal control workers, and the government may cover the cost depending on the province or territory. The pre-exposure vaccine consists of 3 shots given over 28 days: on day 0, day 7, and between day 21 and day 28.

### Post-Exposure Vaccine

A post-exposure vaccine is needed after a bite or suspected rabies contraction. The process differs based on whether the patient already has the pre-exposure vaccine:

- **Without prior vaccination:** 5 shots of the vaccine over 28 days (days 0, 3, 7, 14, and 28), plus 1 shot of rabies immune globulin on day 0. The globulin provides temporary antibodies immediately to begin killing the virus, while the vaccine takes longer to work but eventually lasts longer.
- **With prior vaccination:** No immune globulin needed; only 2 shots of the vaccine on days 0 and 3.

This entire process provides immunity for a few weeks and fully eradicates the rabies virus from the patient's body.

### When to Seek the Vaccine

The rabies vaccine in Canada costs over $200, so it is not a decision to be taken lightly. After any animal injury, always wash and clean the wound first, as this already reduces the possibility of contracting rabies. It is also important to remember that rabies is transmitted through saliva, so a scratch alone is an unlikely way of contracting it. In rare cases, rabies can be passed through the nervous tissue of an infected animal or through air transmission in bat-filled caves.

If there is a chance the animal was rabid, health and animal control can attempt to find and monitor it. When animals begin to carry rabies in their saliva, it only happens for 10 days before they die. If the animal is alive and healthy after 10 days, it was not rabid at the time of the bite. However, this monitoring approach only applies to **cats, dogs, and ferrets**, as the saliva secretion timeline has only been determined for these three animals and is highly variable in others.

For wild animals such as coyotes, foxes, or skunks, if the animal can be captured, it can be tested for rabies. However, a definitive rabies test cannot be performed on a living animal — the animal must be euthanized, and its head sent to a rabies lab immediately. The tissue of the cerebellum and brain stem is examined and produces results within a few days. This is because rabies attacks the nervous system, not the blood, so the infected tissue cannot simply be extracted for a blood test. This is also why humans cannot simply be tested for rabies before deciding on a vaccine.

**If a patient had any direct contact with a bat, they must get the vaccine — no exceptions.** Bats are a reservoir (carrier) for rabies, meaning they can transmit the virus without showing any symptoms. They are difficult to catch and their bites and scratches often go unnoticed due to their small size. If a bat has been found in a room with sleeping people, all individuals must get vaccinated.

### Why Rabies Cannot Be Treated After the Incubation Period

Once neurological symptoms begin, rabies has reached the brain. The brain is protected by the **blood-brain barrier**, a semi-permeable membrane that lets in nutrients while blocking harmful substances. Rabies causes this barrier to essentially "lock down," making it impossible to deliver a vaccine to the infected brain. Some drugs can still pass through, but they cannot be large or water-soluble — and antibodies are blocked entirely.

### Geographic Considerations

The guidelines above apply specifically to Canada. They differ in other countries depending on animal care standards. Canine rabies does not exist in Canada or the U.S., as stray dogs are relatively uncommon and most pets are vaccinated. However, in parts of Asia and Africa, stray animals are very common and contract disease more easily. As a result, most rabies cases in developing countries are caused by dogs, while in Western countries they are caused by bats. Vaccine recommendations and protocols may vary accordingly.

## Alternative Treatment: The Milwaukee Protocol

Despite the insistence that the rabies vaccine is the only way to treat rabies before symptoms start, there is one alternative — though due to its extremely low success rate, it is not considered a standard or safe protocol.

In 2004, 15-year-old Jeanna Giese was bitten by a bat. After a one-month incubation period, she began showing symptoms. Dr. Rodney Willoughby Jr. placed her in a medically induced coma to prevent further brain damage and give her body time to produce antibodies. She was then given antiviral drugs (ketamine, ribavirin, and amantadine) targeting the central nervous system to suppress brain activity. After 6 days she was awoken and later declared rabies-free. She is 37 today.

This became known as **the Milwaukee Protocol** and was reattempted multiple times after the initial success. There have been at least 64 failed attempts and roughly 30 successful ones (the exact number is difficult to confirm). The drugs used in the original protocol did not prove particularly effective in other attempts, and it is possible that Giese had a specific bat strain that was simply easier to treat. The protocol has since been largely abandoned and is not offered as a standard treatment due to its unreliable nature.

## Additional Facts

- There has **never been a reported case** of a human getting rabies from another human. All existing cases have been due to contact with animals. Whether humans truly cannot infect each other, or it is simply a matter of luck, remains unknown.
- **Only mammals can contract rabies.** The virus needs a warm environment to survive, and mammals are warm-blooded. Amphibians, reptiles, and fish are cold-blooded, making their bodies unable to support the virus. Birds, while warm-blooded, are extremely rarely affected — rabies targets neurotrophin, a protein family found in mammals. There is also a theory that birds' higher average body temperature (~40°C) may be too warm for the virus to survive, as mammals' body temperature (~37°C) appears to be its optimal range.
